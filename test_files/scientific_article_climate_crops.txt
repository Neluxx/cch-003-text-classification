Title: Impact of Rising Temperatures on Global Crop Yields: A Meta-Analysis

Abstract:
Rising global temperatures pose a significant threat to agricultural productivity.
This meta-analysis synthesises findings from 147 peer-reviewed studies published
between 2000 and 2024 to quantify the effect of temperature increases on yields
of four major staple crops: wheat, maize, rice, and soybean. Our results indicate
a mean yield reduction of 5.7% per degree Celsius of warming across all crops,
with maize showing the highest sensitivity (7.4% per °C) and rice the lowest
(3.9% per °C). Regional variation is substantial, with tropical and subtropical
regions facing disproportionate losses. These findings underscore the urgency of
adaptive agricultural strategies and accelerated mitigation efforts.

1. Introduction

Global average surface temperatures have increased by approximately 1.2°C above
pre-industrial levels and are projected to rise by a further 1.5–4.0°C by the
end of the century depending on emission trajectories. Agriculture is among the
sectors most exposed to this change, as crop growth, phenology, and water demand
are tightly coupled to temperature regimes.

Previous individual studies have reported mixed results, partly due to differences
in methodology, crop variety, and geographic scope. A quantitative synthesis is
therefore necessary to derive robust, generalisable estimates of temperature
sensitivity across the major food crops that underpin global food security.

2. Methods

We conducted a systematic literature search using Web of Science and Scopus,
combining the terms "crop yield", "temperature", and "climate change". Studies
were included if they reported quantitative yield responses to defined temperature
changes under field or semi-field conditions. A random-effects meta-analytic model
was used to pool effect sizes, with heterogeneity assessed via the I² statistic.

3. Results

Across 147 studies encompassing 2,340 individual observations, warming was
associated with yield reductions in 78% of cases. The pooled effect size
corresponded to a 5.7% yield decline per °C (95% CI: 4.9–6.5%). Substantial
heterogeneity was observed (I² = 73%), explained in part by latitude, irrigation
status, and CO₂ concentration in experimental settings.

4. Discussion

Our findings are consistent with process-based crop model projections but suggest
that yield losses in the tropics may be underestimated in current integrated
assessment models. Adaptation options such as shifting planting dates, developing
heat-tolerant varieties, and expanding irrigation coverage can partially offset
projected losses but are unlikely to fully compensate under high-emission scenarios.
