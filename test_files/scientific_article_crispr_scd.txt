Title: CRISPR-Cas9 Gene Editing for the Treatment of Sickle Cell Disease:
       Clinical Outcomes at 24 Months

Abstract:
Sickle cell disease (SCD) is a monogenic disorder caused by a point mutation in
the HBB gene encoding the beta-globin subunit of haemoglobin. We report 24-month
follow-up data from a phase I/II clinical trial in which 12 patients with severe
SCD underwent autologous haematopoietic stem cell transplantation following
ex vivo CRISPR-Cas9-mediated reactivation of foetal haemoglobin (HbF). Eleven
of twelve patients achieved transfusion independence and showed sustained HbF
levels above 30%. No off-target editing events were detected by whole-genome
sequencing. These results support the therapeutic potential of CRISPR-based
strategies for haemoglobinopathies.

1. Introduction

Sickle cell disease affects approximately 300,000 newborns annually worldwide and
is characterised by chronic haemolytic anaemia, vaso-occlusive crises, and
progressive organ damage. The molecular basis of SCD — a single adenine-to-thymine
substitution at codon 6 of HBB — has been known since 1957, making it an
attractive target for precision gene therapy.

CRISPR-Cas9 has emerged as the most versatile tool for targeted genomic editing,
enabling precise double-strand breaks at defined loci. Prior work demonstrated
that reactivating the silenced gamma-globin genes (HBG1/HBG2) via disruption of
the BCL11A erythroid enhancer increases HbF production sufficiently to ameliorate
SCD pathophysiology in murine and non-human primate models.

2. Study Design

Eligible patients were aged 18–40 years with severe SCD, defined as two or more
vaso-occlusive crises per year requiring hospitalisation despite hydroxyurea
therapy. CD34+ haematopoietic stem and progenitor cells were mobilised, collected
by apheresis, and edited ex vivo using a ribonucleoprotein CRISPR-Cas9 complex
targeting the BCL11A enhancer. Edited cells were cryopreserved and reinfused
following myeloablative busulfan conditioning.

3. Results

Median HbF levels increased from 4.2% at baseline to 38.7% at 24 months. Eleven
patients became transfusion-independent within six months of infusion and remained
so throughout the observation period. One patient experienced delayed engraftment
and required a second infusion of back-up unedited cells. No vector-related
adverse events or malignant transformations were observed.

4. Conclusion

This trial provides the longest follow-up to date for CRISPR-based therapy in SCD
and demonstrates durable HbF induction with an acceptable safety profile. Larger
randomised trials are warranted to confirm these findings across diverse patient
populations.
